PEER REVIEW — "Forecasting Café Ingredient Usage from Heterogeneous Databases with
Hand-Built Exponential Smoothing"
(Garkisch / Martens)

=====================================================================
(1) SUMMARY (including rating)
=====================================================================

The authors demonstrate a forecasting tool that predicts daily usage of
individual café ingredients (milk, sugar, coffee grounds) by joining
transactional sales facts stored in PostgreSQL with drink recipes
stored in MongoDB, and forecasting the resulting per-ingredient time
series with self-implemented exponential smoothing. Two model variants
are provided: Single Exponential Smoothing (SES) as a baseline and
additive Holt-Winters (TES) with weekly seasonality as the default. A
command line interface lets users switch ingredients, choose the model,
and retune the smoothing parameters. Evaluation is taken seriously: a
28-day hold-out backtest reports RMSE, MAE, and MAPE, summarized across
three ingredients in Table I, with the honest takeaway that no single
model always wins (SES marginally beats Holt-Winters on the flat, noisy
coffee-grounds series).

This is a convincing demo paper. The problem is well motivated and
placed in a broader forecasting context, the method is presented with
its update equations and a sound justification for the additive variant
(zero-demand days make the multiplicative form undefined), the setup
section is exemplary (hardware, OS, database versions, Docker Compose),
and the empirical comparison of two self-built models with proper error
metrics clearly exceeds the exercise requirements. The main weaknesses
are formal rather than substantive: the reference list is partly in a
draft-like state (two entries lack authors, year, and bibliographic
details), some design decisions on the recipe-to-ingredient mapping are
not explained, and the text contains a number of language and
consistency slips that a careful editing pass would remove.

Rating: 4 - Accept

=====================================================================
(2) STRONG POINTS (at least two)
=====================================================================

1. Strong, honest empirical evaluation. The paper backs its pitch with
   a 28-day hold-out backtest and reports RMSE, MAE, and MAPE for three
   ingredients (Table I). It openly shows a case where the simpler
   baseline wins (coffee grounds) and explains why MAPE looks inflated
   on low-volume series with near-zero-demand days. This level of
   evaluation transparency is exactly what one hopes for and clearly
   goes beyond the minimum requirement of a visual comparison.

2. Two self-implemented models enabling a real comparison. Providing
   SES as a baseline next to Holt-Winters turns the demo from "one
   algorithm produces a curve" into a small model-selection experiment
   the visitor can perform live (--model ses), which makes the demo
   both more interactive and more instructive.

3. Methodological care. The update equations are given, the additive
   seasonal form is justified by a property of the actual data
   (zero-demand days), the weekly season length is grounded in a
   visible pattern, and the attribution of the method to Holt and
   Winters is historically correct. The concrete detail that the
   database is preloaded with 388 days of sales also matches the
   underlying dataset exactly, which signals careful work.

4. Exemplary setup section. CPU, core count, main memory, OS version,
   PostgreSQL 18 and MongoDB 8, provisioning via Docker Compose, the
   exact Python libraries, and an indication of runtime ("forecasts
   complete in seconds") — this is precisely the reproducibility
   information the template asks for, and the Docker Compose setup
   makes the demo easy to redeploy.

5. Good contextualization. The introduction connects the café example
   to demand forecasting at large with a suitable survey reference
   (Petropoulos et al.), and the two-database design is framed as
   polyglot persistence with the appropriate literature reference
   (Sadalage & Fowler) rather than presented as an unexplained
   artifact of the exercise sheets.

=====================================================================
(3) WEAK POINTS (at least two)
=====================================================================

1. The reference list is partly in a draft state. Entries [1] and [2]
   are incomplete: [1] gives only a title ("Forecasting: Principles and
   Practice (3rd ed)") with no authors (Hyndman & Athanasopoulos),
   publisher, year, or URL, and [2] omits the author entirely although
   the running text explicitly attributes the seasonal extension to
   "Winters [2]" — the reader cannot even confirm from the bibliography
   that [2] is Winters (1960). In contrast, [3] and [4] are fully
   detailed, making the inconsistency conspicuous. Conversely, [4]
   reproduces the complete list of roughly eighty authors verbatim,
   filling an entire column; the customary "et al." after the first
   author(s) would serve readers better. All cited works do exist, so
   this is purely a matter of citation hygiene — but it is the first
   thing a conference reviewer would notice.

2. The recipe-to-ingredient mapping is under-specified. The paper
   states usage(d) is computed from sales counts times per-drink
   ingredient amounts, but does not explain how compound recipe entries
   are handled: in the recipe data, most coffee drinks reference
   "Espresso" in shots rather than listing coffee grounds directly, and
   one recipe offers a choice ("milk or water"). Whether shot entries
   are expanded into base ingredients is not stated, and the strikingly
   small coffee-grounds errors in Table I (RMSE around 5 g/day, versus
   hundreds of ml/day for milk) raise the question of whether the
   coffee-grounds series covers all espresso-based drinks or only the
   few recipes listing grounds directly. Since one of the paper's
   headline findings (SES beats Holt-Winters on coffee grounds) rests
   on this series, the mapping deserves an explicit sentence.

3. Language and consistency issues. Several grammar slips ("Projecting
   it into the future, is a small ...", "can be choosen", "an
   evaluation plot is being generated") and an inconsistent CLI
   description: the text first refers to an --alpha/--beta/--gamma
   parameter style, but the usage example then selects the ingredient
   with "--milk" as its own flag, which is neither explained nor
   consistent with the rest. None of this hurts comprehension badly,
   but the density of slips is noticeable in a two-page paper.

4. Only the future forecast is shown as a figure. The evaluation plot
   (hold-out overlay with error metrics) is described as a key feature
   and would visually substantiate Table I, but it is not included.
   Given that figures may span both columns, showing the backtest
   would have been feasible and more persuasive than describing it.

5. Minor: the implemented Holt-Winters variant uses an undamped linear
   trend, and the demo projects 30 days ahead. A brief remark on how
   the forecast behaves over longer horizons (or why damping was not
   needed) would preempt an obvious question. Likewise, the imputation
   of days without sales is only hinted at in the architecture figure
   caption ("imputes") and never specified in the text.
